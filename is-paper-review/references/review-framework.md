## 1. Phenomenon & Significance

**Core questions:**
- Does the paper address a phenomenon that is important to IS scholars and/or practitioners?
- Is the research problem clearly stated and compelling?
- Does the introduction convince the reader that this problem is worth solving?
- Is the IS relevance explicit (not assumed)?

**Strong:** Problem is timely, consequential, and clearly situated in the IS landscape. Motivation includes both theoretical gap and practical relevance.

**Weak:** Motivation is thin ("few studies have examined X"), problem is tangential to IS, or the paper treats IS significance as self-evident.

**Fatal:** The phenomenon is trivial, already well-understood, or irrelevant to the IS community.

**Reviewer questions to ask:**
- Why should IS scholars care about this specific problem now?
- Does the paper distinguish between a topic (what it is about) and a phenomenon (why it matters)?
- Is there a meaningful research gap, or is this gap manufactured?

## 2. Originality & Contribution

**Core questions:**
- What specifically does this paper contribute that did not exist before?
- Is the contribution clearly articulated (theory, method, phenomenon, design)?
- Is the contribution meaningful in magnitude, not merely incremental?
- Does the paper advance IS knowledge or merely replicate prior work in a new context?

**Types of IS contributions:**
- **Theory**: New theory, model, or significant theoretical extension
- **Empirical**: New insights from novel context, data, or methodology
- **Methodological**: Novel research methods or instruments
- **Design**: New artifact with demonstrated utility
- **Synthesis**: Integration of fragmented literature with novel insights

**Strong:** Contribution is explicit, novel, and positioned clearly against specific prior work.

**Weak:** Contribution is described only vaguely ("this paper fills a gap"); contribution is to "test theory X in context Y" without additional insight.

**Fatal:** The paper's core claims already appear in prior published work; contribution is only incremental replication.

**Reviewer questions to ask:**
- What would we know after reading this paper that we didn't know before?
- Is the delta from prior work clearly articulated and meaningful?
- Is this a "first study of X" contribution, and if so, is that sufficient?

## 3. Journal Fit

**Core questions:**
- Is the scope and audience appropriate for this venue?
- Does the paper align with the journal's epistemological and methodological orientation?
- Is the IS disciplinary identity clear (vs. management, computer science, sociology)?

**IS top journals and their orientations (indicative):**
- **MIS Quarterly**: Broad IS scope; theory-rich empirical and conceptual; innovation welcome
- **ISR**: Rigorous empirical; strong theory; both positivist and interpretivist
- **JMIS**: Managerial implications prominent; both technical and behavioral IS
- **JAIS**: Methods pluralism; explicitly welcomes diverse approaches
- **EJIS**: European perspective; sociotechnical, interpretive traditions strong
- **MISQ Executive**: Practitioner-facing insights from research

**Strong:** The paper's questions, theory, and method fit naturally within the journal's scope and tradition.

**Weak:** Paper belongs to a neighboring discipline; IS framing is superficial or bolted on.

**Fatal:** Paper makes no meaningful IS contribution and would fit better in a CS, management, or sociology journal without modification.

## 4. Theoretical Foundation

**Core questions:**
- Is an appropriate theoretical lens used, developed, or tested?
- Is the theory applied correctly, not just cited?
- Are the warrants (assumptions underlying the theoretical claims) justified?
- For theory-building papers: is the new theory internally consistent and testable?

**Dimensions to evaluate:**
- **Appropriateness**: Is the chosen theory the best lens for this phenomenon?
- **Depth**: Is theory engaged substantively or merely mentioned?
- **Correctness**: Is the theory applied accurately, consistent with its original formulation?
- **Development**: Does the paper extend, adapt, or challenge theory in a novel way?
- **Coherence**: Do theoretical assumptions align with research design choices?

**Strong:** Theory is central to the paper's argument; its use is justified over alternatives; theoretical contribution is explicit.

**Weak:** Theory is applied mechanically without justification; theory is a veneer over a purely empirical paper; key theoretical assumptions are violated.

**Fatal:** Theoretical claims are internally contradictory; theory is fundamentally misapplied; theoretical and empirical elements are disconnected.

**Reviewer questions to ask:**
- Would a different theoretical lens provide better insight? If so, why was this one chosen?
- Are the core constructs grounded in theory or ad hoc?
- Does the paper engage with theoretical debates, or treat theory as settled?

## 5. Methodology

**Core questions:**
- Is the research design appropriate for the research questions?
- Are procedures described with sufficient detail to assess rigor and enable replication?
- Is the unit of analysis clear and appropriate?
- Are the choices (method, sample, analysis) justified, not just described?

**By paradigm — see [methodology-guides.md](methodology-guides.md) for detailed checklists.**

**General criteria:**
- Research questions ↔ method alignment: quantitative for variance theories; qualitative for process theories
- Sampling strategy justified and appropriate
- Data collection procedures described in sufficient detail
- Analysis procedures documented and appropriate
- Validity/reliability (or trustworthiness/credibility) addressed explicitly

**Strong:** Method is well-justified; procedures are transparent; validity threats are identified and mitigated.

**Weak:** Method is assumed, not justified; procedures lack detail; validity is treated as a box-ticking exercise.

**Fatal:** Research design cannot answer the stated research questions; fundamental methodological errors (e.g., common method bias not addressed in self-report studies; no member checking in interpretive work).

## 6. Data Quality & Validity

**Core questions:**
- Are the data sources credible and appropriate?
- Do the data actually measure/capture what the paper claims?
- Are findings intelligible, interesting, and believable?
- Do results support the conclusions, and only the conclusions, drawn?

**Quantitative validity threats to check:**
- Construct validity: Do measures capture the intended constructs?
- Internal validity: Are causal claims warranted by design?
- External validity: Are generalizability claims appropriate for the sample?
- Statistical conclusion validity: Are tests appropriate and power sufficient?
- Common method variance: Is CMV tested and addressed?

**Qualitative trustworthiness criteria:**
- Credibility (internal validity equivalent): member checks, triangulation
- Transferability (external validity): thick description, purposive sampling rationale
- Dependability (reliability): audit trail, protocol documentation
- Confirmability (objectivity): reflexivity, alternative interpretation consideration

**Strong:** Data quality is explicitly established; validity threats are systematically addressed; claims match evidence.

**Weak:** Validity is asserted rather than demonstrated; sample characteristics poorly described; claims subtly exceed evidence.

**Fatal:** Data cannot support the claims; fundamental measurement errors; results are not reproducible from described procedures.

## 7. Logical Correctness

**Core questions:**
- Are all inferences logically sound?
- Is the chain of reasoning from evidence to conclusion coherent?
- Are mathematical/statistical operations correct?
- Are there hidden assumptions that, if wrong, would invalidate the conclusions?

**Common logical errors in IS papers:**
- Affirming the consequent (finding consistent with theory ≠ confirms theory)
- Ecological fallacy (group-level findings applied to individuals)
- Causal claims from cross-sectional data
- Cherry-picking evidence while ignoring disconfirming cases
- Overgeneralizing from limited samples

**Strong:** Conclusions follow logically from evidence; alternative explanations are considered and addressed.

**Weak:** Logical leaps are present but minor; conclusions are somewhat overstated.

**Fatal:** Fundamental logical fallacies undermine the core argument; causal claims with no causal design.

## 8. Literature Coverage

**Core questions:**
- Are seminal IS works in this area cited and engaged (not just listed)?
- Are recent developments (last 3–5 years) represented?
- Is the literature review analytical (synthesizing, positioning) rather than merely descriptive?
- Are works cited accurately (no misrepresentation of sources)?

**Red flags:**
- Absence of key foundational papers that the paper clearly builds on
- Literature review that describes each paper in isolation without synthesis
- Over-reliance on textbooks rather than primary sources
- Citation of papers that contradict the current paper's claims, without engagement
- Reference list padding (citing tangentially related work to appear comprehensive)

**Strong:** Literature review identifies a clear gap, positions the paper's contribution precisely, and engages critically with key debates.

**Weak:** Literature is listed but not synthesized; contribution positioning is vague; some key papers are missing.

**Fatal:** Core papers in the area are missing; the paper's contribution is undermined by existing work the authors appear unaware of.

## 9. Limitations & Future Work

**Core questions:**
- Are the boundaries of the study's claims honestly and specifically articulated?
- Are limitations substantive (methodological, contextual) rather than perfunctory?
- Do future research directions follow logically from the paper's findings and limitations?

**What good limitations look like:**
- Specific, not generic ("single industry context" rather than "generalizability may be limited")
- Explain why the limitation exists and what it means for interpretation
- Suggest how future work could address the limitation

**Reviewer guidance:**
- Do not penalize papers for having limitations — all research has them
- Penalize papers that have obvious limitations and do not acknowledge them
- Do not require authors to conduct additional studies to address every limitation

## 10. Presentation & Clarity

**Core questions:**
- Is the paper well-organized with a clear, logical flow?
- Is language precise, consistent, and free of ambiguity?
- Are tables, figures, and examples appropriate and helpful?
- Is the paper written at a professional standard (grammar, style)?

**Dimensions:**
- **Structure**: Abstract → Introduction → Literature → Theory → Method → Results → Discussion → Conclusion flows logically; each section serves its purpose
- **Clarity**: Key constructs defined precisely and used consistently throughout
- **Coherence**: Argument built cumulatively; no major gaps in the narrative
- **Concision**: Claims stated directly; no unnecessary repetition
- **Visuals**: Tables and figures add information, not just repeat text

**Strong:** Paper is a pleasure to read; argument is immediately clear; figures enhance comprehension.

**Weak:** Writing requires effort to parse; terminology inconsistent; some structural issues slow reading.

**Fatal:** Paper is fundamentally unclear; reviewers cannot determine what the paper claims or how it supports claims.

**Note on presentation vs. substance:** Presentation issues alone should not sink a paper with excellent contributions. Flag clearly, but distinguish from substantive issues.
