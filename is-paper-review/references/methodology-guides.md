## 1. Quantitative Empirical Research

### Research Design Checklist

**Research Questions & Hypotheses**
- [ ] Research questions are clearly and precisely stated
- [ ] Hypotheses are derived from theory (not post hoc)
- [ ] Direction of hypothesized relationships is justified theoretically
- [ ] Research questions and hypotheses are aligned

**Constructs & Measurement**
- [ ] All key constructs are theoretically defined
- [ ] Operationalization follows established scales where available, or new scale development is rigorously validated
- [ ] Items are appropriate for the construct and population
- [ ] Content validity established (expert review, pilot testing)
- [ ] Convergent validity (AVE ≥ 0.50; loadings ≥ 0.70)
- [ ] Discriminant validity established (HTMT < 0.90 or Fornell-Larcker criterion)
- [ ] Reliability established (Cronbach's α ≥ 0.70; CR ≥ 0.70)

**Sample & Data Collection**
- [ ] Sample frame is appropriate for the research context
- [ ] Sample size is adequate (power analysis or established guidelines)
- [ ] Sampling procedure described (random, convenience, snowball, etc.)
- [ ] Response rate reported and non-response bias addressed (if survey)
- [ ] Data collection procedure described in sufficient detail
- [ ] IRB/ethics approval addressed

**Common Method Variance (CMV)**
- [ ] CMV assessed procedurally (temporal/spatial separation, different response scales)
- [ ] CMV assessed statistically (Harman's single factor, marker variable, CFA-based approaches)
- [ ] Impact of CMV on findings discussed

**Analysis**
- [ ] Statistical method appropriate for data structure and research questions
- [ ] Model fit assessed with appropriate indices (for SEM: CFI/TLI > 0.95, RMSEA < 0.08, SRMR < 0.08)
- [ ] Mediation tested appropriately (bootstrapped indirect effects, not Baron & Kenny steps)
- [ ] Moderation tested with appropriate interaction terms (not subgroup comparison)
- [ ] Controls justified theoretically and empirically
- [ ] Endogeneity addressed for causal claims (IV, Heckman, PSM)
- [ ] Effect sizes reported (R², Cohen's f², β)

**Interpretation**
- [ ] Conclusions match the strength of the design (correlation ≠ causation unless experimental)
- [ ] Statistical significance not conflated with practical significance
- [ ] Null results interpreted appropriately (not proof of no effect)
- [ ] Competing explanations considered

### Common Quantitative Paper Weaknesses
- Hypotheses not grounded in theory; appear to be post hoc
- CMV dismissed with single Harman factor test (insufficient)
- Causal language used with cross-sectional data
- Sample characteristics not reported in adequate detail
- Effect sizes omitted; statistical significance over-interpreted
- Magic sample size numbers (n=200) without power analysis
- Discriminant validity issues overlooked

## 2. Qualitative Empirical Research

### Research Design Checklist

**Research Questions & Methodology**
- [ ] Research questions appropriate for qualitative inquiry (process, meaning, context)
- [ ] Qualitative tradition stated and appropriate (case study, grounded theory, ethnography, action research, phenomenology)
- [ ] Methodology is appropriate for the stated tradition (not all qualitative is the same)
- [ ] Epistemological stance stated (interpretivist, critical, pragmatic)

**Research Site & Access**
- [ ] Site selection justified (theoretical, not just convenience)
- [ ] Access and rapport described
- [ ] Participant selection rationale provided (purposive sampling logic)
- [ ] Participant demographics/characteristics described appropriately

**Data Collection**
- [ ] Data sources described in detail (interviews, observations, documents, artifacts)
- [ ] Interview protocol described (semi-structured, open-ended)
- [ ] Data collection duration and scope appropriate for depth of claims
- [ ] Field notes, recordings, transcription procedures described
- [ ] Data saturation addressed (for inductive approaches)

**Analysis**
- [ ] Analysis procedure described step-by-step (coding, categorizing, theorizing)
- [ ] Coding approach described (open/axial/selective; thematic; interpretive)
- [ ] Multiple coders or inter-rater reliability addressed (if applicable)
- [ ] Analytic induction vs. constant comparison made clear
- [ ] Evidence presented (quotes, vignettes, examples) supports claims

**Trustworthiness (Lincoln & Guba criteria)**
- [ ] **Credibility**: Member checking, triangulation, prolonged engagement
- [ ] **Transferability**: Thick description, purposive sampling rationale
- [ ] **Dependability**: Audit trail, consistent analysis procedures
- [ ] **Confirmability**: Reflexivity statement, alternative interpretation considered

**Theory**
- [ ] Role of theory stated upfront (sensitizing concepts, inductive, abductive)
- [ ] Theoretical contribution articulated clearly
- [ ] Findings linked back to theory or theoretical implications developed

### Common Qualitative Paper Weaknesses
- Research questions look quantitative (test/examine/measure) rather than qualitative (explore/understand/how/why)
- Inadequate thick description of research context
- Code/category development not described; analysis is a black box
- Evidence (quotes) cherry-picked; disconfirming evidence ignored
- Member checking and other trustworthiness criteria absent
- Generalizability confused — qualitative generalizes theoretically, not statistically
- Theory is absent or superficially applied post hoc

## 3. Design Science Research

### Research Design Checklist (Hevner et al. 2004 criteria)

**Problem Relevance**
- [ ] Problem is grounded in an important practical or organizational challenge
- [ ] Problem is justified with evidence (not assumed to be important)
- [ ] IS relevance is clear

**Design Artifact**
- [ ] Artifact clearly described and categorized (construct, model, method, instantiation)
- [ ] Artifact is novel (not merely implementing known solutions)
- [ ] Design rationale explained (why these design decisions)
- [ ] Artifact boundary conditions and assumptions stated

**Design Evaluation**
- [ ] Evaluation strategy clearly described before reporting results
- [ ] Evaluation criteria operationally defined
- [ ] Evaluation method appropriate for artifact type (case study, experiment, simulation, expert opinion)
- [ ] Artifact evaluated against requirements/objectives
- [ ] Limitations of evaluation addressed

**Research Contributions**
- [ ] Contribution to IS knowledge stated explicitly (beyond the artifact itself)
- [ ] Design principles or kernel theories articulated
- [ ] Design knowledge generalizable beyond this specific artifact

**Communication**
- [ ] Targeted to both technologist and management audiences
- [ ] Artifact described with sufficient detail for replication or use

### Common Design Science Weaknesses
- Artifact presented as engineering contribution without knowledge contribution
- Evaluation is weak (self-assessment by authors only)
- Design choices not justified; alternatives not considered
- "IS relevance" is assumed, not argued
- Contribution conflated with the artifact itself (artifact ≠ knowledge)

## 4. Conceptual / Theoretical Papers

### Research Design Checklist (Hirschheim 2008)

**Introduction & Motivation**
- [ ] Topic and its importance clearly established
- [ ] Audience identified; motivation appropriate for that audience
- [ ] Structure of paper explained upfront

**Claims & Thesis**
- [ ] Core thesis/argument is explicitly stated (not left to the reader to infer)
- [ ] Claims are clearly and specifically articulated throughout
- [ ] Thesis is novel and advances IS discourse beyond existing frameworks

**Literature Engagement**
- [ ] Relevant literature is comprehensive and well-synthesized
- [ ] Paper is positioned explicitly against key prior work
- [ ] Contribution distinguished from related frameworks/theories
- [ ] Literature engaged critically, not just described

**Argumentation**
- [ ] Arguments are logically coherent and built cumulatively
- [ ] Evidence (empirical or theoretical) supports each major claim
- [ ] Assumptions (warrants) are made explicit and justified
- [ ] Reasoning is penetrating — goes beyond surface-level analysis
- [ ] Alternative views are considered and addressed

**Theoretical Contribution**
- [ ] New theory/framework is internally consistent
- [ ] Constructs and relationships are clearly defined
- [ ] Theory is falsifiable/testable (or value of unfalsifiable framework justified)
- [ ] Practical implications follow logically from theoretical claims

**Structure & Unity**
- [ ] Paper flows logically with clear section transitions
- [ ] Nothing important is left out; no significant redundancy
- [ ] Each section serves the paper's overall argument

### Common Conceptual Paper Weaknesses
- Thesis buried or never explicitly stated
- Literature review is descriptive, not analytical
- Arguments are asserted rather than reasoned through
- Assumptions (warrants) hidden and unjustified
- Framework is not clearly differentiated from existing models
- Contribution is vague ("this paper offers a new perspective")
- No practical or research implications derived from theory

## 5. Mixed Methods Research

### Research Design Checklist

**Rationale for Mixed Methods**
- [ ] Rationale for combining methods is explicit and compelling
- [ ] Mixed methods design type stated (sequential explanatory, sequential exploratory, convergent)
- [ ] Each component contributes distinctively to the research objectives

**Integration**
- [ ] Point and manner of integration described (data-level, interpretation-level, reporting-level)
- [ ] Findings from quantitative and qualitative strands are meaningfully integrated
- [ ] Contradictions between strands addressed, not ignored

**Component Quality**
- [ ] Quantitative component meets quantitative standards (see Section 1)
- [ ] Qualitative component meets qualitative standards (see Section 2)
- [ ] Integration does not sacrifice rigor of either component

**Common Mixed Methods Weaknesses**
- Methods run in parallel with no actual integration
- One component trivially supports the other without adding insight
- Rigor of one component sacrificed for the other
- Mixed methods used to compensate for weaknesses in a single-method design
