## Tone and Style Guidelines

**Do:**
- Be specific — reference page numbers, section headings, specific claims
- Be constructive — every major criticism should be paired with a suggestion
- Be balanced — acknowledge strengths and identify genuine issues
- Be proportionate — distinguish fatal flaws from fixable problems
- Be collegial — write as a senior colleague giving honest, kind feedback

**Avoid:**
- Vague criticism without specific evidence ("the theory is weak")
- Demanding changes that do not add essential value
- Demanding additional studies to address every limitation
- Ghostwriting — do not rewrite the paper into the study you'd like to see
- Janus-faced assessment — inconsistency between author and editor comments
- Dismissive language ("this paper is simply wrong / not ready for publication")
- Raising new issues in revision reviews that should have been raised in round 1

## Example: Strong vs. Weak Comments

**Weak comment:**
> "The literature review is inadequate. Many important papers are missing."

**Strong comment:**
> "Issue 3: Incomplete engagement with TAM extensions (p. 4–7). The literature review positions the paper as extending the Technology Acceptance Model but does not engage with Venkatesh et al.'s (2003) UTAUT or Venkatesh et al.'s (2012) UTAUT2, which directly address the social influence and hedonic motivation constructs central to this paper. Without engaging these works, the paper's contribution claim — that it offers a novel extension — cannot be evaluated. The authors should position their model explicitly against UTAUT/UTAUT2 and clarify what their model adds beyond these established frameworks."

## Revision Review Template

When reviewing a revision, structure your response as follows:

1. **Overall assessment of revision**: Has the paper improved? Are major issues resolved?
2. **Response to major issues** (go through each numbered issue from original review):
   - Issue resolved / Partially resolved / Not resolved — with brief explanation
3. **New issues arising from the revision** (if any; keep to essential new concerns only)
4. **Recommendation**: Based on progress, updated recommendation

**Important:** Do not introduce major new concerns that should have been raised in the first round. Focus on whether prior concerns are adequately addressed.
